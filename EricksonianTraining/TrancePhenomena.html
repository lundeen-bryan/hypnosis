Deep Trance Phenomena — Reference

Descriptive catalog (not a protocol)

Scope: Observable and subjective phenomena that may occur in deep hypnosis. Occurrence varies by individual, context, and learning history. Depth increases availability; it does not guarantee any item.

Motor & Muscular Phenomena

Examples of movement, tone, or posture changes.
  • Catalepsy (rigidity or immobility)
  • Lethargy (relaxation/heaviness)
  • Automatic movements
  • Hand levitation
  • Postural fixation
  • Spontaneous micro-movements
  • Motor dissociation (movement without conscious intent)
  • Ideomotor signaling (e.g., finger yes/no)

Sensory & Perceptual Phenomena

Alterations in sensation or perception.
  • Analgesia (reduced pain)
  • Anesthesia (absent sensation)
  • Hyperesthesia (heightened sensation)
  • Selective sensory blocking
  • Altered body image (size/weight/position)
  • Kinesthetic distortion
  • Visual distortion
  • Auditory distortion
  • Tactile illusion

Cognitive & Experiential Phenomena

Shifts in attention, appraisal, or thought flow.
  • Time distortion (compression/expansion)
  • Absorption
  • Reduced critical evaluation
  • Heightened focus / narrowed attention
  • Spontaneous insight
  • Non-linear or associative thinking
  • Symbolic thinking / metaphoric experience
  • Reduced executive monitoring
  • Automatic cognition

Memory Phenomena includes Regression

Changes in access, organization, or perspective.
  • Hypermnesia (enhanced recall)
  • Hypomnesia (reduced recall)
  • Amnesia (temporary/selective)
  • Age regression
  • Age progression / future projection
  • State-dependent recall
  • Memory re-contextualization
  • Dissociation from autobiographical memory

Dissociative Phenomena

Separation or partitioning of processes or identity.
  • Ego dissociation
  • Observer perspective
  • Depersonalization
  • Derealization
  • Split awareness
  • Parts-based experience
  • Automatic self-regulation
  • Compartmentalization

Autonomic & Physiological Phenomena

Shifts mediated by autonomic or reflex pathways.
  • Respiratory pattern changes
  • Heart rate variability changes
  • Thermal shifts (warmth/coolness)
  • Vasodilation / vasoconstriction
  • Reduced startle reflex
  • Altered muscle tone
  • Tearfulness without distress
  • Salivation or swallowing changes

Emotional & Affective Phenomena

Modulation independent of external context.
  • Affect modulation
  • Emotional numbing
  • Emotional amplification
  • Context-free emotion
  • Emotional detachment
  • Emotional re-association
  • Automatic affect regulation

Identity & Self-Concept Phenomena

Temporary changes in self-model or role.
  • Temporary role adoption
  • Identity softening
  • Reduced self-referential processing
  • Flexible self-concept
  • Future-self identification
  • Symbolic identity expression

Post-Trance & Carryover Phenomena

After-effects or delayed expressions.
  • Preservation of response
  • Post-hypnotic ideomotor activity
  • Delayed insight
  • Behavioral generalization
  • Residual time distortion
  • Spontaneous problem re-framing
  • Somatic symptom change without direct suggestion

Real-Life Analogies & Everyday Examples

Concrete situations that mirror listed phenomena. These are common experiences and do not imply formal hypnosis.
  • Ideomotor / Hand levitation: Reaching up to scratch an itch before consciously deciding; the arm rises toward the face “on its own.”
  • Catalepsy: Holding a phone or book for a long time and noticing stiffness or immobility only after attention shifts.
  • Absorption & Time distortion: Losing track of time while reading, gaming, or watching a movie.
  • Selective sensory blocking: Not noticing background noise when deeply focused; “tuning out” conversation in a café.
  • Analgesia: Not feeling a small cut until seeing it; reduced pain during intense exercise or emergencies.
  • Auditory/visual distortion: Music seeming louder or softer depending on focus; lights feeling bright after a dark theater.
  • Amnesia / Hypomnesia: Forgetting where keys were placed; walking into a room and forgetting the purpose (doorway effect).
  • Hypermnesia: A forgotten name or code suddenly surfacing later without effort.
  • Regression-like state access: A childhood song or smell automatically changing mood, voice, or posture.
  • Observer perspective (dissociation): Mentally replaying a conversation while seeing oneself from the outside.
  • Highway hypnosis: Driving on autopilot and realizing you’ve reached your exit with minimal conscious recall.
  • Autonomic shifts: Heart rate increase or blushing when seeing someone attractive; goosebumps from powerful music.
  • Automatic affect regulation: Calming after deep breaths without consciously deciding how to calm down.
  • Identity softening / role adoption: Feeling “like a leader” or “like a parent” when stepping into a familiar role or uniform.
  • Post-event insight: A solution appearing suddenly during a shower or walk, after stopping deliberate thinking.

Clarifications: (1) “Spontaneous” refers to emergence without direct suggestion; it is still shaped by context and expectancy. (2) Depth increases probability, not certainty. (3) Phenomena are signals or resources, not outcomes by themselves.
