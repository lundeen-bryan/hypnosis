O’Hanlon — Class of Problems → Class of Solutions

A practical, Ericksonian-flavored decision tree. Preserve what works. Invite flexibility.
Quick start
1. Name the complaint: concrete, observable words.
2. Classify the problem: excess, deficit, instability, attempted-solution, frame mismatch, or secondary gain.
3. Choose solution class: keep the function, change the form; amplify exceptions; stabilize; do different; change frame; protect gain.
4. Invite small differences: earlier noticing, pausing, or letting it pass.
5. Future test: imagine the next cue and notice how the new response can happen naturally, easily.
6. Utilization: weave any spontaneous responses as evidence the system already knows how.
1 Is this an excess or an absence complaint?
Excess = “too much of X” (urge, worry, pain, behavior). Absence = “not enough Y” (confidence, sleep, focus).
Excess → Class: Reduce/Redirect (Keep the function, change the form)
  • Preserve the intention (e.g., stress relief) while inviting easier expressions.
  • Language: “And stress can settle sooner now… without the old form.”
Absence → Class: Elicit/Increase (Install opportunities & exceptions)
  • Find exceptions, scale them, rehearse in trance, seed cues and prompts.
  • Language: “And you can notice where this already happens… and let it happen more.”
2 Does performance vary? (Sometimes better, sometimes worse)
If yes, you already have exceptions. Turn them into a protocol.
  • Class: Stabilize/Regulate — capture what’s different in the “better times” (context, sequence, internal cues).
  • Scale “better” to make it repeatable; anchor or ritualize the sequence.
  • Language: “And the way it goes better sometimes… can become the usual way.”
3 Are the client’s attempted solutions maintaining the problem?
Common traps: fighting urges, over-monitoring, avoiding life, reassuring loops.
  • Class: Do Something Different — stop what keeps it going; invite a gentler, shorter, or opposite move.
  • Paradox options (when appropriate): symptom prescription, “try to do it slightly,” time-limit the pattern.
  • Language: “You might do just enough to notice… you don’t need to.”
4 Is there a frame mismatch? (Goal/measure/context off)
E.g., expecting zero stress; measuring by urges rather than choices; wrong timescale.
  • Class: Change the Frame — redefine success (earlier noticing, softer urges, quicker settling).
  • Use scaling, exceptions, and time horizons that fit how change really happens.
  • Language: “Success can be noticing sooner and letting it pass more easily.”
5 Is there secondary gain or a job the symptom does?
If the behavior protects/benefits something, don’t fight it — protect the gain differently.
  • Class: Protect the Function — keep the benefit (soothing, focus, break) and offer alternative forms.
  • Language: “Keep the relief; let the old method be optional.”

Reference — O’Hanlon’s Six Classes of Problems and Solutions

Use this table to quickly match a client’s presenting issue to the appropriate class of problem and its corresponding therapeutic move.

Class of Problem Meaning Matching Solution Class
1. Excess Too much of something — behavior, emotion, pain, worry, craving. Reduce / Redirect — keep its function, change the form.
2. Absence / Deficit Not enough of a desired quality — confidence, calm, motivation. Elicit / Increase — find exceptions, amplify them.
3. Instability / Variation Works sometimes but not always. Stabilize / Regulate — identify and ritualize conditions of success.
4. Attempted-Solution Problem The client’s way of fixing it maintains the issue. Do Something Different — stop or reverse the maintaining attempt.
5. Frame Mismatch Goal or scale is misdefined — unrealistic or misframed. Change the Frame — redefine success in achievable, process-based terms.
6. Secondary Gain Symptom serves another purpose — protection, comfort, attention. Protect the Function — keep the benefit, change the form.
Session skeleton (5 steps)
  1. Align — validate the job the symptom does; agree to protect it.
  2. Classify — excess / absence / instability / attempted-solution / frame / gain.
  3. Invite — low-load suggestions matching the solution class.
  4. Rehearse — future pace one tiny difference; anchor exceptions.
  5. Verify — client marks their next-cue plan in 8–10 words.
