“That’s right.” — A Tiny Power Tool in Ericksonian Hypnosis

One-line summary

“That’s right” = Notice a helpful change → acknowledge it → invite the next small step.

Practice drills (5–10 minutes)

From The Deep Trance Training Manual by Igor Ledochowski

  1. Ask your partner to put themselves in a trance. Allow them to close their eyes or self-induce in their own comfortable way.
  2. Once they’re in trance, Spot & Mark: Observe your partner’s micro-signs for 60–90 seconds. Only say “That’s right” when something changes.
  3. Once they’re in trance, Breath-sync: Time “That’s right” to their exhale for two minutes.
  4. Once they’re in trance, Link-forward: After “That’s right,” add a soft bridge: “and…” + gentle direction. Keep it under 7 words.
    Examples: “and you might notice your breathing continue to slow,” “and that comfort can spread a little further,” “and you may start to drift even deeper.”

A quick, plain-language guide you can read or keep beside your scripts.

What it means (in simple terms)

“That’s right” is a gentle acknowledgment. You’re telling the person, “What you’re doing now is good. Keep going.” It’s not a command. It’s a warm green light.

Why it works

  • Reinforces helpful responses (heteroaction): When they sigh, breathe deeper, or settle, you softly mark it with “That’s right,” and the body-mind continues in the same direction.
  • Marks trance signs (trance analogues): Eye flutters, slower blinking, stillness, facial softening — “That’s right” tells the unconscious these shifts are welcome.
  • Builds safety and flow: It validates experience without pushing. The session feels natural, permissive, and smooth.
Think of it as a verbal nod. You notice something good; you nod; they continue.

When to say it

  • After any spontaneous, useful change (a deeper exhale, eyelids heavier, settling posture).
  • Right after they follow a suggestion (they close their eyes, count down, imagine a scene).
  • When you want to bridge from pacing to a next step.

How to say it

  • Tone: Warm, low, unhurried.
  • Timing: Immediately after the helpful sign.
  • Pacing: Let a micro-pause come before it so it lands.
  • Volume: Slightly softer than surrounding words.
Pro tip: Let the breath out as you say it. Your exhale invites their exhale.

Voice qualities for “That’s right”

  • Tone: Warm, approving, and permissive — as if gently confirming something that’s already happening.
  • Pitch: Let it fall slightly on the word right to convey calm certainty. Avoid upward or questioning tones.
  • Rhythm: Match your partner’s tempo or breathing. Say it on their exhale if possible. Include a small pause before and after.
  • Volume: Softer than surrounding words — about half-volume, not a whisper. Whisper only if it feels natural and the environment allows intimacy.
Lower pitch + slower rhythm + soft volume + warm tone = hypnotic pacing.

Adapting your voice through the session

At the start, during conversation or suggestibility tests, your voice can be brighter, faster, and more animated to match the person’s alert state and build rapport. In these moments, saying “That’s right!” with light enthusiasm can pace and lead them toward a feeling of energy, interest, and confidence about the process.

This early excitement helps replace uncertainty with engagement and readiness. As trance responses appear, gradually slow your rhythm, lower your pitch, and soften your tone so that “That’s right” becomes a gentle, validating marker.

The shift from lively pacing to calm guiding mirrors the subject’s journey — you begin where they are and naturally lead them where they’re going.

Example uses (plug-and-play)

Induction (pacing → leading): “Noticing the simple rhythm of your breathing… that’s right… and with each exhale, comfort can spread a little more.”

Deepening (mark a sign, link it forward): “As the eyelids get just a bit heavier… that’s right… and with that, drifting down another level now.”

Imagery/therapy: “As you picture that calmer scene beginning to form… that’s right… and you can allow the feeling that comes with it to settle in.”

Utilizing a spontaneous response (heteroaction): “There’s that longer breath… that’s right… your system knows exactly how to continue.”

Pacing resistance: “You don’t have to go any faster than you’re going… that’s right… just letting it unfold in the way that’s most comfortable for you.”

Common mistakes (and quick fixes)

  • Mechanical or too often: Use it selectively at meaningful moments. Let silence do the rest.
  • Forcing progress: Keep it non-directive. You’re validating, not pushing.
  • Flat or rushed delivery: Slow down. Drop your voice slightly. Allow a micro-pause before/after.
  • Premature marking: Notice → Pause → “That’s right.” → Link forward.

A tiny formula you can memorize

Notice the useful sign → Acknowledge it (“That’s right…”) → Link it to the next gentle direction.

Mini-scripts you can drop into sessions

  • “And as your attention naturally turns inward… that’s right… allowing comfort to grow.”
  • “Feeling the shoulders lower just a touch… that’s right… and with that, more ease arrives.”
  • “Even if part of you is just curious… that’s right… curiosity can open space for change.”
  • “You don’t have to make anything happen… that’s right… simply letting what’s already happening continue.”

Troubleshooting

  • They look “stuck”: Mark any subtle analogue (a blink, stillness). “There… that’s right… noticing even that tiny shift is enough.”
  • They open their eyes or fidget: Normalize and utilize. “Wandering is natural… that’s right… and as you settle again, the next breath can take you deeper inside.”
  • You feel unsure what to say: Quietly watch and wait for the next analogue, then mark it.

Ethical note

Use “That’s right” to support autonomy. It validates their experience. Avoid using it to override boundaries or push an agenda.
