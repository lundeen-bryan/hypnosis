Demystifying Hypnosis — Pre-talk Handout

Use the short pretalk to reframe hypnosis, then choose one metaphor card and address common concerns before inviting the experience.

Pretalk
Start here: prior experience
“Have you ever been hypnotized before?”
If yes: “How was that experience for you?” (Listen. Note preconceptions, positives, concerns.)
If no: “Great — we’ll keep it simple and natural. I’ll guide you step‑by‑step.”
Intro you can read verbatim
“Hypnosis for most people is what they see on a stage show, where they see someone say ‘SLEEP!’ and then they go to sleep, but hypnosis isn’t sleep, it’s a heightened state of focus called a trance. And we’re in a trance most of the time. For example…”
Metaphor Openers
How many windows are in your house?
“As you picture each one, your mind drifts naturally into imagination — the same kind of focus that makes hypnosis so effective.”
Have you ever been driving and realized you missed your exit because your mind drifted?
“That’s the same kind of natural focus your mind uses in hypnosis.”
Have you ever been so absorbed in a book or movie that you lost track of time?
“That absorption is similar to how trance feels.”
Have you ever caught yourself daydreaming and noticed the world fading into the background?
“That gentle drifting is the same kind of experience we use in hypnosis.”
Have you ever listened to music so deeply that everything else seemed to disappear?
“That focused absorption is perfect for this process.”
Have you ever found yourself in the zone while walking or exercising, where your body moves almost automatically?
“That comfortable rhythm is the same kind of focus we’ll use now.”
Have you noticed those moments just before sleep when thoughts wander and images appear on their own?
“Hypnosis is like that in‑between state — aware, yet pleasantly absorbed.”
Have you ever been so engaged in a conversation or hobby that time seemed to fly by?
“That natural time‑distortion is common in trance as well.”
Bridge into Induction Example: “Yes? Great — that’s exactly the kind of focus your mind can enjoy right now, and as you notice your breathing, you can just let your eyes close… and begin to relax more deeply.”
Addressing Common Concerns
There are usually three common concerns people have about hypnosis that I hope to clarify first.
Use a calm, factual tone. Normalize each question before you reassure.
“What if I get stuck inside and can’t get out?”
“Hypnosis is a state of focus — not sleep. You remain aware, and you can open your eyes or move anytime you wish.”
“What if I reveal secrets while in hypnosis?”
“You stay in control of what you say. Your mind protects your privacy the same way it does in normal conversation.”
“What if I do something silly or weird?”
“In therapeutic hypnosis, you’re always in charge of your behavior. The process simply helps your mind respond more easily to positive suggestions.”
Lemon imagination exercise (before Q&A)
“If you like, you can close your eyes. Picture a fresh lemon on a cutting board — notice the bright skin and the weight in your hand.”
“Imagine slicing it open… hear the knife, see the juice bead up and catch the scent as it lifts.”
“Bring a wedge to your lips and imagine the sharp, sour taste. Notice what your mouth is doing now.”
Why this works: It primes vivid sensory imagination and natural body responses, making later suggestions easier.
Invite Questions: “Do you have any questions about hypnosis?”
Outcome & Focus for Today
Outcome question (ask briefly, listen fully)
“Wonderful. Now that you know what hypnosis really is, what would you like to get out of today’s experience?”
“It could be something to improve, feel more confident about, or a habit you’d like to change.”
Reflect and prime: Briefly echo their words in positive terms (e.g., “So you’d like to feel calm and focused more often — excellent.”) Then bridge to induction.
Answer questions now: Invite and address any remaining questions before the concentration exercise. Keep responses brief, reassuring, and oriented to their goal.
Transition to Induction
Chosen invitation
“We can do a simple exercise — not a test or a challenge, just a way to notice how your mind naturally creates focus.”

Tip: One metaphor is usually enough. If it doesn’t land, try a different one — then bridge with and.
