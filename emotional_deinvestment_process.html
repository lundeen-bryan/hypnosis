Emotional De-Investment Practice

Before/After Self-Check

Before a session: Ask yourself:

  • How emotionally reactive do I feel toward Lee right now (1–10)?
  • Am I focused on proving something or practicing something?

After the session: Ask:

  • How calm and self-directed do I feel (1–10)?
  • What’s one thing I did today that shows my own growth?

1. Reframe the Relationship

Shift your mental story from “I need Lee to grow” to “Lee is one of several tools I use to grow.” Think of him as a practice prop, not an emotional source. He’s there for skill repetition, not validation or friendship.

2. Reduce Emotional Hooks

Before sessions: remind yourself, “This is practice, not personal.” After sessions: write only the facts about what worked or what to adjust. Don’t replay tone or emotions.

3. Control Access

Limit contact to what’s necessary for practice. No chatting or venting. It prevents emotional re-attachment.

4. Redirect Validation

After each session, ask:

  • What did I do right?
  • What improved since last time?
  • What do I want to refine next?

Share wins with someone who offers genuine encouragement.

5. Expect Emotional Lag

Old attachment patterns may linger. When you crave acknowledgment, say quietly: “That’s the old pattern — I can self-acknowledge now.”

6. Optional Daily Exercise (3 Minutes)

  1. Take one deep breath in and out.
  2. Recall one skill you practiced today.
  3. Say to yourself: “I’m proud of how I showed up today.”
  4. If Lee comes to mind, remind yourself: “He’s part of the exercise, not part of my identity.”
  5. Smile softly and release the thought.

7. Releasing the Need to Win

When the mind replays arguments where you were right and he was wrong, pause and remind yourself:

“Winning the debate won’t bring peace. Mastery will.”

Redirect that energy into growth:

  • What skill or quality do I want to demonstrate so clearly that no one could deny it?
  • How can I embody my growth instead of proving it?

The true victory is internal — confidence that no longer depends on others’ validation. You don’t need to win; you just need to outgrow the game.
